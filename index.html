First decision

Do you need an appraisal, an estate sale, or an auction?

Use an appraisal when the value has legal, tax, insurance, estate, donation, or family-settlement consequences. Use an estate sale when the goal is to sell a broad household collection efficiently. Use an auction when competitive bidding, scarce items, or specialist buyers may matter more than a fixed local sale.

Appraisal

Best for documented value: fair market value, replacement cost, probate files, charitable donations, insurance schedules, and division of property.

Estate Sale

Best for tangible personal property across a home: furniture, jewelry, clothing, decorative arts, kitchenware, tools, books, toys, and collectibles.

Live Auction

Best for grouped lots, stronger buyer competition, unusual objects, art, jewelry, advertising pieces, silver, and collections with clear provenance.

Useful starting checklist

Before anything is sold, photographed, donated, or packed

1. Separate documents and personal material

Pull passports, tax files, medical papers, family photographs, letters, military records, passwords, checkbooks, and anything with private identity information before buyers or clean-out crews enter the house.

2. Photograph rooms before sorting

Wide room photos preserve context. Then photograph individual objects with scale, signatures, labels, hallmarks, maker marks, damage, and any boxes or receipts.

3. Flag items that need research

Set aside art, silver, jewelry, watches, signed pottery, early toys, old advertising, books, manuscripts, military items, rugs, musical instruments, and anything with family provenance.

4. Match value type to purpose

Donation value, replacement value, estate value, auction estimate, and quick-sale value are not the same thing. The wrong value type can mislead heirs or create tax problems.

Research-backed notes

Facts that changed the rebuilt content

The old site contained useful antique and estate-sale themes, but the rebuilt pages now add current reference points: IRS fair-market-value language, qualified appraisal report elements, USPAP appraisal standards, estate-sale definitions, and preservation advice for family papers and photographs.

FMV is not insurance replacement value

For donated property, IRS guidance centers fair market value on an open-market willing buyer and seller. It also warns that insurance replacement value may not reflect donation FMV.

A good appraisal explains the method

Useful reports identify the property, date of value, appraiser qualifications, value conclusion, valuation method, and the basis for that conclusion, such as comparable sales.

Estate sales are tangible-property events

Public tax guidance describes an estate sale as a sale or auction of tangible personal property, often household goods, for a deceased person or a living person who is moving.

Preservation starts before repair

The National Archives emphasizes preventing damage to family papers and photographs, which is why the guides recommend separating documents before the sale process begins.
