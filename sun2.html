Estate sale guide

Estate Sale Liquidation Services Guide

The old Midnight Sun estate-sale page listed antiques, collectibles, furniture, jewelry, glass, pottery, silver, and household contents. This rebuild turns that topic into a usable planning guide.

What an estate sale is

An estate sale is usually a sale or auction of tangible personal property: household goods, furniture, jewelry, clothing, decorative objects, tools, books, toys, and similar items. It can follow a death, a move, a downsizing, or another need to clear property from a home.

The practical goal is not just to sell things. A good liquidation process protects private papers, identifies objects that need appraisal, presents saleable goods clearly, and leaves a documented record of what was sold, donated, kept, or discarded.

Estate sale preparation sequence

  1. Secure the home. Remove documents, medications, firearms, financial records, passwords, checkbooks, family photographs, and anything with identity information before public access.
  2. Photograph each room. Take wide shots before moving objects, then detail shots of signatures, labels, hallmarks, damage, drawer contents, backs, undersides, and original boxes.
  3. Sort by value risk. Put aside jewelry, silver, watches, art, rugs, old toys, advertising, books, coins, stamps, military items, instruments, and signed ceramics for closer review.
  4. Choose sale format. Common items may fit a house sale; specialist categories may need auction, consignment, or separate appraisal before pricing.
  5. Plan the final disposition. Decide in advance what happens to unsold goods: family holdback, donation, clean-out, recycling, or specialist sale.

Pricing and appraisal cautions

Do not use one number for every purpose. A quick sale price, estate sale price, auction estimate, fair market value, and insurance replacement value can all differ. If heirs need a defensible record, get an appraisal before objects are mixed into bulk sale inventory.

For donation planning, IRS guidance treats fair market value as an open-market value between informed willing parties. It also distinguishes that from insurance replacement value, which can be higher and may not represent donation value.

What to keep out of the sale until reviewed

  • Fine jewelry, watches, silver flatware, hollowware, coins, stamps, and small valuables.
  • Paintings, prints, sculpture, photographs, signed pottery, rugs, and framed documents.
  • Early toys, trains, dolls, advertising pieces, cameras, books, manuscripts, autographs, and ephemera.
  • Military items, medals, uniforms, instruments, rare tools, and items with family provenance.

Post-sale recordkeeping

Keep a simple spreadsheet with category, description, condition notes, sale method, sale price, retained items, donations, and discarded items. This helps heirs understand what happened and gives an executor or family organizer a clean reference file.
