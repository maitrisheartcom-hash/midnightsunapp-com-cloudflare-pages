Live Auction Preparation Guide

The archived live-auction page mixed useful auction context with injected spam and old contact data. This rebuild keeps the useful part: how to prepare antiques and collectibles for a live sale.

When a live auction makes sense

A live auction can work well when buyer competition matters: art, jewelry, silver, collectibles, advertising, grouped box lots, unusual furniture, estate collections, and items where local estate-sale pricing may be too shallow. It is less useful for ordinary household goods unless they are grouped efficiently.

Seller preparation

  • Create clear lot groups: single high-value items, matched sets, themed box lots, and ordinary household lots.
  • Provide condition notes and photographs before preview.
  • Keep provenance, receipts, appraisals, certificates, and restoration records with the lot.
  • Decide whether reserves are appropriate and whether they are realistic for the venue.
  • Ask how unsold lots, buyer premiums, seller commissions, pickup deadlines, and disputes are handled.

Buyer-facing information

Buyers need clear descriptions, visible condition issues, lot contents, measurements, and preview access. For box lots, list the best visible contents rather than writing vague labels such as miscellaneous. For art and jewelry, include marks, signatures, medium, dimensions, metal marks, and any documentation.

Absentee and phone bids

Live auctions often allow absentee or phone bids. Sellers should understand how bids are recorded, whether maximum bids are confidential, how tie bids are handled, and when payment and pickup occur. Buyers should inspect condition before bidding whenever possible.

Catalog hygiene

Do not let spammy injected text, old toolbars, or broken external scripts into a rebuilt catalog page. A clean static page should contain the lot context, condition language, and internal navigation only.
