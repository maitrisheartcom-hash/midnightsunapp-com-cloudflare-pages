Mixed Estate Sale Inventory Guide

The archived page listed antique furniture, china, bottles, jewelry, toys, cameras, art, books, pottery, advertising, vintage clothing, appliances, tools, and garage contents. This guide explains how to sort that kind of mixed estate.

Use three passes

  1. Safety and privacy pass: remove private documents, medications, weapons, cash, family photos, keys, and financial records.
  2. Value-risk pass: pull jewelry, silver, art, watches, old cameras, books, toys, advertising, military items, and signed pottery for review.
  3. Sale setup pass: group ordinary goods by buyer category and price only after the higher-risk items are separated.

Categories from the archived inventory

The old inventory included furniture, china, primitives, bottles, jewelry, watches, toys, cameras, prints, lithographs, books, magazines, character collectibles, lighting, pottery, collector plates, advertising items, trunks, uniforms, vintage clothing, kitchenware, appliances, and tools. That mix is typical of a full-house estate.

What can hide value

  • Books with inscriptions, limited editions, maps, plates, or dust jackets.
  • Old cameras with lenses, cases, manuals, or brand-specific accessories.
  • Advertising signs, trays, tins, thermometers, and store displays.
  • Uniforms, medals, patches, photographs, and service records.
  • Garage tools with maker marks, early design, or industrial use.

Pricing discipline

A mixed estate can look overwhelming, which encourages bulk pricing too early. Price common goods in lots, but keep researched categories separate. If the estate has hundreds of tools or books, sample the better items first instead of averaging the whole room.
